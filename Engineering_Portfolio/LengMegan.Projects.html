Project Name: MIT App Inventor project- Photobooth

Project Date: December 2015- January 2016 for CSE course

Course: CSE

Partner(s): Christy Koh

The objective of this project was to improve a premade app on MIT App Inventor, a program similar to Scratch that also works with drag and drop blocks. I worked with my partner Christy Koh to modify the provided code to produce a working, personalized photobooth app. Completing this project allowed me better understand variable roles and applications. Although we did come accross a few problems during the building, we were able to overcome these challenges and improve our app. One such challenge was the orientation/stretching of images. When we first tested the given code, we discovered that the pictures came out rotated 90 degrees and stretched to fit the canvas. In order to remedy this, we chose to switch to my Samsung tablet. We were soon able to catch up despite having spent multiple days resolving the camera function issue. Teamwork and constructive criticism both played a very important role in this project; the teamwork and helpful comments were what ultimately enabled us to successfully complete the project. Project Name: Machine Control Design- Delivery Vehicle

Project Date: May 1, 2017 - May 5, 2017

Course: POE

Partner(s): Wesley Wong, Allen Nguyen, Mounica Padakandla

The objective of this project was to use select one of a variety or provided problems and use VEX robotics and programming to develop a solution. To do this, our team first agreed upon a problem to solve, then each brainstormed until we came up with a design for a robot that would accomplish the task required. We then discussed as a team the pros and cons of each design, and used a decision matrix to select one design to build. Our robot was supposed to solve the problem of transporting parts from one location to another; it consisted of a cup-like structure with wheels that moved back and forth on a track. The "cup" used a single motor for power and utilized a potentiometer to change direction, as well as a number of bump switches and limit switches to start, emergency stop, and control the movement process. My main responsibilities in this project were using ROBOTC to program the machine and assisting with the building and documentation, so I was able to gain a significant amount of knowledge in using ROBOTC and how the programming actually affected the robot's actions. As a team, we had many obstacles to overcome, most notably the effectiveness of our transporting cup and the direction changing process. To make our cup better, we wrapped tracks around our initial single gear platform design to ensure that it could successfully hold objects, and to fix our direction changing problem, we attached 1/2 inch standoffs to the bottom of our cup to ensure that the bump switches on either end of the track would be able to register that the cup had reached the end. As this project emphasized teamwork and the division of group responsibilities for efficiency, I was able to demonstrate my communication skills and cooperation by explaining how the code I wrote worked to the rest of our team, and making sure everyone had a good understanding of all aspects of our project, including the parts they were not directly responsible for. Project Name: PCB Design

Project Date: October 2018

Course: DE

Partner(s): None

The objective of this project was to create a printed circuit board (PCB) from scratch, starting with the circuit design and finishing with a functional board. To do this, I began by designing my circuit in Multisim and tested it on a breadboard to ensure it worked. My circuit was supposed to make an LED "pulse" with a breathing effect; I'd taken inspiration from my Halloween costume and wished to design a light-up prop. The circuit consisted of two 555 timer sections, one to regulate the blinking and another to control the fade-out of the LED. Because this was an individual project, I was responsible for first designing and testing the circuit, using Fritzing to get the printed version, and masking/etching the design onto the board before finally drilling holes and soldering in the components. The masking and etching process proved particularly difficult, however, because my circuit (though I followed all instructions) refused to mask properly, and the copper traces would be missing/broken after the etching process despite the initial transfer going well. Another issue I encountered was the actual PCB not functioning. I tried making solder bridges and replacing components I suspected were faulty, but ultimately was unable to fix the board due to time restraints. Despite my final product not functioning as intended, I was still able to gain important experience with the actual PCB production process, as well as gain hands-on practice with soldering to enhance my skills.
